Winston Churchill

The Greatest Man of the 20th Century

A Chronology of the Great Man's Life

  • Born: 30 November 1874, Woodstock, Oxfordshire, UK, son of Lord Randolph Henry Spencer-Churchill and Lady Randolph Churchill, n√©e Jerome, an American from the great borough of Brooklyn, New York.
  • Graduated Harrow: 1893, after writing his mother many times that he couldn't stand the place.
  • Commissioned: 20 February 1895, Cornet, 4th Queen's Own Hussars, after graduating from the Royal Military College, Sandhurst.
  • Bombay: October, 1896, stationed in Bombay, British India.
  • Battle of Omdurman: 2 September 1898, participated in the last meaningful British cavalry charge as a member of the 21st Lancers under General Herbert Kitchener.
  • War Correspondent: 12 October 1899, when the Second Boer War broke out, he became a war correspondent for the Washington Post. He was later captured by the Boers and escaped to much fanfare.
  • Parliament: 1900 General Election, seat of Oldham, Churchill entered Parliament.
  • Married: 12 September 1908, Church of St. Margaret, Westminster Abbey, City of Westminster, London, UK, to Clementine Hozier.
  • First Lord of the Admiralty: 1911 - 1915, he received this appointment before World War I and continued in it until the failure of the Gallipoli landings in the Dardanelles led to his ouster.
  • Chancellor of the Exchequer: 1924-1929, he oversaw a return to the gold standard that apparently didn't go as well as it should have.
  • First Lord of the Admiralty: 3 September 1939 - 10 May 1940, he became a member of Neville Chamberlain's War Cabinet.
  • Prime Minister: 10 May 1940 - 26 July 1945, he began his first term as P.M. under attack from the Nazis, and the country in disarray, and left with the defeat of the Axis powers and the country restored.
  • Opposition Leader: 1945 - 1951,
  • First Stroke: 1949, on holiday in the south of France, he suffered his first stroke.
  • Prime Minister: 26 October 1951 - 6 April 1955, he began his second term as P.M. during the early years of the Cold War when western values were under attack by Soviet communists and British socialists, and did his best to fight these swamp rats, but it is an ongoing struggle.
  • Nobel Prize: 1953, won the Nobel Prize for Literature.
  • Died: 24 January 1965, Kensington, County London, UK, aged 90 of a stroke.

Major Historical Works

  • The World Crisis: 1923 - 1931, six volumes.
  • Marlborough: His Life and Times: 1933 - 1938, four volumes on his ancestor.
  • The Second World War: 1948 - 1953, six volumes.
  • A History of the English-Speaking Peoples: 1956 - 1958, four volumes.

Major Speeches

  • Blood, Toil, Tears, and Sweat: 13 May 1940.
  • We Shall Fight on the Beaches: 4 June 1940.
  • Their Finest Hour: 18 June 1940.
  • Never Give In: 29 October 1941.
  • The Sinews of Peace: 5 March 1946.
